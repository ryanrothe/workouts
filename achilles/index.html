What this program is doing — and why

This 12-week program is built around the two interventions with the strongest evidence for Achilles tendon adaptation, plus a structured progression into reactive loading so your tendon can store and return energy under basketball-specific demands.

The three loading types — what each does

1. Heavy Isometric

30–45s holds at high effort. Cook & Rio's work shows isometrics reduce tendon pain and increase tendon stiffness via collagen reorganization. Lower joint-stress than dynamic loading.

2. Heavy Slow Resistance (HSR)

Slow tempo (3-0-3) at 70–85% 1RM. Kongsgaard and Beyer trials show HSR produces tendon hypertrophy and stiffness gains equal to or better than eccentrics, with better adherence.

3. Plyometric / Energy Storage

Pogos → bounding → depth jumps. Trains the tendon's stretch-shorten cycle. Without this, return-to-sport doesn't replicate basketball loads.

4. Eccentric Overload

Alfredson-style step heel raises with 4-sec lowering. Long-established for tendon remodeling — kept as a maintenance dose, not the main driver.

Why both gastroc and soleus

The soleus actually transmits more force through the Achilles during running and jumping than the gastroc, but it's almost always undertrained because most calf work is done with straight knees. Every day in this program includes a bent-knee soleus loading drill. This is the most commonly missed piece of post-rupture programs.

How to load: RPE and progression

  • RPE 7–8 = 2–3 reps left in the tank. This is your working zone for HSR sets.
  • RPE 9 = 1 rep left, or you couldn't hold another 5 seconds. This is your isometric target.
  • Add ~2.5–5% load each week if all prescribed reps were clean at target tempo.
  • Some tendon "warmth" or soreness up to ~4/10 during loading is fine. Sharp focal pain, swelling, or 24-hr morning stiffness is a signal to back off 10–15%.
The Silbernagel pain rule (well-validated): Pain ≤ 5/10 during the exercise, and back to baseline by the next morning. If both true, the load is fine and you can progress.

Why 3 days/week

Tendons adapt slowly — collagen turnover is a 24–72 hour process. Loading more often than every other day on the same tissue gives diminishing returns and risks accumulating microtrauma faster than you can remodel it. Three sessions with full recovery in between is the sweet spot for tendon work in someone training other things (like basketball) on off-days.

Basketball alongside this

  • Schedule heavy tendon sessions (Day A, B) not on the same day as basketball.
  • Day C can stack with light shooting/skill work, but avoid full-court play immediately after.
  • If you played hard the day before, downgrade an isometric day to a deload (60% volume).

Phase logic

1
Weeks 1–4 — Foundation. Build tendon stiffness with heavy iso + HSR. Light plyo prep only.
2
Weeks 5–8 — Energy Storage. Maintain HSR, layer in bilateral and controlled single-leg plyometrics. Multi-directional intro.
3
Weeks 9–12 — Reactive / Sport-Specific. Stiffness plyos (min ground contact), depth drops, single-leg reactive, bounding. Basketball-ready.

Self-Assessment — re-test every 4 weeks

Track these once at Week 1, then re-test at the end of each phase (Weeks 4, 8, 12). The symmetry number is what matters most — you're rebuilding the injured side to match the uninjured side.

How to test: Warm up with 2 min jump rope. Rest 90s between each test. Test injured leg second (so you're not pre-fatigued the wrong way).

Test 1 — Single-Leg Heel Raise to Fatigue

Stand on one leg, fingertips on wall for balance. Full ROM heel raise to a 2-sec hold at top, lower fully. Count reps until form breaks. Repeat on other side.

Test 2 — Single-Leg Hop for Distance

From standing, hop forward on one leg as far as possible and stick the landing for 3 sec. Best of 3. Measure heel-to-heel.

Test 3 — Single-Leg Heel Raise Load Test

Hold heaviest DB you can do 5 strict single-leg heel raises with, off a step, full ROM. Conservative — go light first time.

Limb Symmetry Index (LSI): Injured ÷ Uninjured × 100. Most return-to-sport literature uses ≥ 90% as the threshold for full clearance to high-impact activity. Below 80% = stay conservative with plyo dosing this phase.
